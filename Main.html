What is the Ozone Layer?

The ozone layer is a crucial part of Earth's stratosphere, located about 15 to 35 kilometers above the surface, that contains a high concentration of ozone (O₃) molecules. It plays a vital role in protecting life on Earth by absorbing the majority of the sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation, particularly UV-B rays, which can cause skin cancer, cataracts, and damage to ecosystems. The depletion of this layer, primarily due to human-made chemicals like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), has raised global concern, prompting international efforts such as the Montreal Protocol to mitigate further damage and ensure the recovery of the ozone shield.

How Does the Ozone Layer Protect Us?

The ozone layer acts as Earth's sunscreen. It absorbs about 98% of the Sun’s ultraviolet light, particularly harmful UV-B and UV-C rays, which can cause severe damage to living organisms. By blocking these harmful rays, the ozone layer plays a crucial role in protecting all life forms on Earth.
